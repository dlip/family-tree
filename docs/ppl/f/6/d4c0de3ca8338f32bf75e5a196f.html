A story of the times.
Posted 20 Sep 2015 by margmartin53

http://mv.ancestry.com.au/viewer/0d91ae75-adaf-4a65-9e5f-f00e633f8869/26004359/1764651168

William Willie and Louisa had their first child out of wedlock, for which Louisa was kept in secret for around 6 months. The baby was born premature and died soon after. She was still living in her mothers house, and her father had visitors, and when he saw her in the kitchen area - he quickly removed his guests to the front verandah. When they had left he struck her so hard, she fell forwards and overnight lost the baby. William Willie was also born under the same rule and was punishment in front his siblings; by standing in absolution for a period of one day in total without water. After the death of his and Louisa first child, he had to win the right to marry Louisa, which required him to have one of his hands tied behind his back - and fight against her father. After they were married, Louisa found it difficult to ever forgive her father - because of his denial in ever having caused the death of her first born. 'He telling everyone, it was her fault in neglecting to care for herself properly, being too young to be sired.'

Louisa's mother, in punishing her children, used a willow whip across the back of the legs of the girls and a sixer of the top face of the hands on the males. A sixer; being six cuts of the cane. She was to administer the punishment whilst he stood in watch as witness - to be to his satisfaction. The duration of the pain was considered a reminder of the wrong they did to deserve it. So even though he was considered upstanding in his community; didn't mean he was a man worthy of his status behind the scenes.
Another interesting fact to note, is the arranged marriages of the time also - as can be seen on my grandmothers marriage certificate (Rosetta Victoria) you will note that it was written by the minister, and not signed by either of the bride or groom, only in witness by the father of the bride. The difference in the other writing was by the mother of Frederick, because of where the marraige was held; in her house. Neither bride or groom were permitted to sign prior to being legally in agreement, if the marriage was pre arranged by the father.

William was smitten with Frederick Richard Martin because of his friendship, and after the marriage was consumated; Frederick - drunk one day, struck William Willie with a closed fist and knocked him down. William forbid him to cross the threshold of his home again. So it was that Grandma could not visit for long lengths of time, for which she felt she too was being punished by the actions of a man not of her choosing.
It is also not to take away from William Willie who was a strict Presbyterian and lived by his word, 'the child before me, is more then the wife who bore the child; that my name be theirs in testament - whilst she be the bearer in labour in all that they know is good, and bad in her raising of them, not me, but those born of me.' He wasn't as strict as the church might like to have enforced upon its followers, and after his mayorship, he became a much gentler man - he himself saying one day of his regret in not understanding sooner that to repeat the sins of the father, was something he came to relise the worst time of his life..and so felt better when he gave up his belief in the church of which he referred to as religious ritualistic fanaticism. Louisa was of the church, and aided in raising money for the poor and underprivilaged on top of all her other duties. She was hard on the outside and as my grandmother would say; broken.

19 Feb 1891 – District Intelligence - Orange
A Dastardly Trick. — On Friday night at about half-past eight, a handsome cab, driven by a man named Curtis Carr, was proceeding up the incline in William street, East Orange, just opposite Lack's hotel, the harness suddenly got lose, and the cab tilted right up, the back of the driver's seat striking the ground. Inside the cab were Mr. W. Pullbrook and his little girl, neither of whom fortunately were hurt. The driver was stunned for the time, but otherwise escaped. The horse ran away, but was soon re-captured. Mr. Lack and some other persons, seeing the accident, hastened to the spot and righted the vehicle. It was found that the harness was uninjured, and it was clear that the belly-band, saddle band, and hame straps had been unfastened by some would-be funny person with a reckless disregard of such tom-foolery.
